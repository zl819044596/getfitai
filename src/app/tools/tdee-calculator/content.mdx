# TDEE Calculator: Calculate Your Total Daily Energy Expenditure

Understanding how many calories your body burns each day is the foundation of any successful fitness journey. Whether you want to lose fat, build muscle, or maintain your current weight, knowing your TDEE is essential.

## What is TDEE?

**Total Daily Energy Expenditure (TDEE)** represents the total number of calories your body burns in a 24-hour period. It accounts for everything from basic bodily functions to intense physical activity.

Your TDEE is composed of four main components:

### 1. Basal Metabolic Rate (BMR)
This is the energy your body needs to maintain basic life functions — breathing, circulating blood, cell production, and organ function. BMR typically accounts for **60-75%** of your total calorie burn.

### 2. Thermic Effect of Food (TEF)
Your body burns calories digesting, absorbing, and processing the food you eat. TEF accounts for approximately **10%** of TDEE, with protein requiring the most energy to process.

### 3. Exercise Activity Thermogenesis (EAT)
Calories burned during structured exercise like weightlifting, running, or sports. This varies dramatically based on your training intensity and frequency.

### 4. Non-Exercise Activity Thermogenesis (NEAT)
All the movement outside of formal exercise — walking, fidgeting, standing, typing, even chewing gum. NEAT can vary by up to 2,000 calories between individuals.

## How to Calculate TDEE

The most accurate method uses the **Mifflin-St Jeor Equation** to estimate BMR, then multiplies by an activity factor:

### Step 1: Calculate BMR

**For men:**
BMR = (10 × weight in kg) + (6.25 × height in cm) - (5 × age in years) + 5

**For women:**
BMR = (10 × weight in kg) + (6.25 × height in cm) - (5 × age in years) - 161

### Step 2: Apply Activity Multiplier

| Activity Level | Multiplier | Description |
|---------------|-----------|-------------|
| Sedentary | 1.2 | Little to no exercise, desk job |
| Lightly Active | 1.375 | Light exercise 1-3 days/week |
| Moderately Active | 1.55 | Moderate exercise 3-5 days/week |
| Very Active | 1.725 | Hard exercise 6-7 days/week |
| Extremely Active | 1.9 | Physical job + hard training |

### Step 3: Adjust for Goals

- **Lose weight:** TDEE - 300 to 500 calories
- **Maintain weight:** Eat at TDEE
- **Build muscle:** TDEE + 200 to 300 calories

## Why TDEE Matters

### For Fat Loss
Eating below your TDEE creates a calorie deficit, forcing your body to use stored fat for energy. A sustainable deficit of 300-500 calories typically results in 0.5-1 pound of fat loss per week.

### For Muscle Gain
Eating above your TDEE provides the extra energy needed for muscle protein synthesis. A modest surplus of 200-300 calories minimizes fat gain while supporting growth.

### For Maintenance
Knowing your TDEE helps you maintain your current physique without unwanted fluctuations.

## Common TDEE Mistakes

1. **Overestimating activity level** — Most people are less active than they think
2. **Not recalculating** — TDEE changes as weight, age, and activity change
3. **Ignoring NEAT** — Steps and daily movement matter significantly
4. **Being too aggressive** — Extreme deficits lead to muscle loss and metabolic adaptation

## How to Use Your TDEE Results

1. **Track your intake** for 2 weeks using your calculated TDEE
2. **Monitor your weight** — If it doesn't change, your TDEE estimate is accurate
3. **Adjust by 100-200 calories** if weight moves in the wrong direction
4. **Recalculate monthly** as your body composition changes

## TDEE vs. BMR: What's the Difference?

| | BMR | TDEE |
|---|-----|------|
| Definition | Calories burned at complete rest | Total calories burned in 24 hours |
| Activity | None accounted for | All activity included |
| Use case | Medical calculations | Diet planning |
| Typical value | Lower | 1.2-1.9x BMR |

## Frequently Asked Questions

**How accurate is TDEE calculation?**
TDEE calculators provide estimates within 10-15% of actual expenditure. Individual variations in metabolism mean you may need to adjust based on real-world results.

**Should I eat back exercise calories?**
If using a TDEE calculator with activity factored in, don't add exercise calories separately — they're already included. If using a sedentary TDEE, you can add estimated exercise burn.

**Why did my TDEE decrease?**
As you lose weight, your body requires fewer calories. A 10-pound loss typically reduces TDEE by 50-100 calories. This is normal and requires periodic recalculation.

**Can I boost my TDEE?**
Yes! Build muscle (muscle burns more calories than fat), increase daily steps, eat more protein (higher TEF), and consider HIIT training for the afterburn effect.
